Week 2: Welfare v. Duty: Utilitarian and Deontological Perspectives on Ethics
Objectives
Managerial domain: Non-market strategy challenges in the global economy
This session addresses common business situations by stipulating moral principles (act and rule utilitarianism) and deriving ethically defensible behavior. We examine rule utilitarianism as an approach to handle situations where individual actions might lead to bad collective outcomes, gaining an appreciation for the algorithmic nature of ethical reasoning.
Managerial application: Human Resources
We will confront gender asymmetries in organizational life using two frameworks:
Consequentialist Ethics (Utilitarianism): Seeking to maximize the well-being of a specified group (similar to public policy or business optimization).
Deontological Ethics (Duty-based): Connecting with strong intuitions regarding rights and duties.
Preparation Guidelines
How would a utilitarian evaluate the dilemma facing Ziqitza's leadership?
"When in Rome, do as the Romans do." Should you always play by local rules? Does the answer change between Act vs. Rule Utilitarianism?
Is it right to sacrifice the individual for the collective? Always, sometimes, or never?
Do businesses have a responsibility to create an inclusive work environment if it doesn't maximize welfare or profits?
Should romantic relationships among members of the same organization be forbidden? Why or why not?

Week 4: Environmental Justice and the Ethics of Training Generative AI
Objectives
Managerial application: Inequality, equity, sustainability
In this integrative class, we discuss two contemporary issues through the lens of our frameworks:
Environmental Justice: We examine market-based mechanisms (like pollution rights) which are technically successful but often lead to asymmetric outcomes across social groups.
Generative AI: We explore the trade-off between amassing the massive amounts of data needed for effective AI models and respecting the property rights of content creators.
Preparation Guidelines
What are the motives of opponents/proponents of market mechanisms to resolve environmental challenges? Do their claims have a rigorous ethical defense?
Are market-based mechanisms discriminatory? Are they fair?
Assess the fairness of the pollution credits trading system. Would your assessment differ if you lived in a hotspot?
Whose property rights are at stake in the debate over training generative AI models and IP?
Would an act and rule utilitarian agree or disagree about the ethics of training generative AI models without licensing agreements in place?

Week 5: Corporate Social Responsibility and Gene Editing
Objectives
Managerial application: Corporate Social Responsibility, ethical challenges from scientific progress
This session explores ethical corporate leadership and the definition of CSR. We will contrast traditional leadership with leadership in economic ecosystems and address the thorny issue of who "owns" the firm. These frameworks will be applied to the case of CRISPR and gene editing—technologies that could redefine what it means to be human.
Preparation Guidelines
Should firms do everything they can to “do good,” only certain things, or none?
Do shareholders own firms, and should managers maximize share value?
Do you think CSR can be defined meaningfully beyond legal compliance? If so, what would be a good definition?
Consider the situation from Dr. He’s position. What paths of action are available? What are the individual risks and rewards involved?
Would a virtuous choice involve other considerations? Which?
Who are the relevant actors, and what are the stakes involved in pursuing gene editing?
